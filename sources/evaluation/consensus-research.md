# **Empirical and Proxy Country Rankings for Erin Meyer's "Evaluating" Scale (Direct vs. Indirect Negative Feedback)**

## 1. Introduction

The "Evaluating" scale from Erin Meyer's "The Culture Map" distinguishes cultures by their preference for **direct** versus **indirect negative feedback** in professional and social contexts. To empirically rank countries on this scale, research was prioritized as follows: (1) quantitative country-level scores that can be mapped or normalized to a 0–99 scale, (2) qualitative comparative data that can be interpreted into relative positions, and (3) proxy metrics from adjacent constructs such as assertiveness, individualism-collectivism, face-saving, and high/low-context communication. The most robust quantitative proxies are the GLOBE project's Assertiveness dimension (62 countries), Gelfand's Tightness-Looseness index (33 countries), Uz's Cultural Tightness Index (68 countries), and Hofstede's Individualism scores. Qualitative and mixed-method studies provide direct comparisons of criticism strategies across cultures (e.g., US vs. Korea, Japan vs. Minangkabau, Iraq vs. Malaysia), while face-negotiation theory and high/low-context frameworks offer additional interpretive tools  (House et al., 2002; Bonjeer & Vonkova, 2023; Fullerton et al., 2025; Haristiani et al., 2023; Hosseinizadeh & Moqadam, 2019; Uz, 2015; Safari, 2020; Ting-Toomey et al., 1991; Gelfand et al., 2011).

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Quantitative Country-Level Indices

- **GLOBE Assertiveness Scores:** The GLOBE project provides the most directly mappable quantitative data on assertiveness—a strong proxy for direct negative feedback—across 62 societies  (House et al., 2002; Bonjeer & Vonkova, 2023; Terlutter et al., 2010; Rafferty, 2025; Dorfman et al., 2012; Shi & Wang, 2011). Assertiveness is measured on a 1–7 scale; higher scores indicate more direct communication styles.
    - Example: Egypt has the lowest assertiveness score among all GLOBE countries  (Moneim, 2007).
    - US, Germany, Austria typically score high; East Asian nations like Japan and Korea score low  (House et al., 2002; Terlutter et al., 2010).
- **Tightness-Looseness Index:** Gelfand et al.'s tight-loose culture index ranks 33 nations by norm enforcement strength; tighter cultures tend toward indirect communication to avoid confrontation  (Gelfand et al., 2011). Uz's index extends this to 68 countries using SD-based measures  (Uz, 2015).
- **Hofstede Individualism:** High individualism correlates with more direct feedback; collectivist societies favor indirectness  (Bonjeer & Vonkova, 2023; Akaliyski et al., 2025; Minkov & Kaasa, 2022).

### 3.2 Qualitative & Mixed-Method Comparisons

- Studies comparing criticism strategies show Americans use more direct negative feedback than Koreans or Persians; Japanese prefer indirect strategies compared to Minangkabau speakers who are more direct  (Haristiani et al., 2023; Hosseinizadeh & Moqadam, 2019; Safari, 2020; Ali, 2022).
- In Asian medical education settings, negative feedback is often delivered indirectly due to face-saving concerns and hierarchical norms  (Fullerton et al., 2025).
- Peer feedback studies in international classrooms reveal students from high-context cultures struggle with the direct style favored in low-context cultures like the Netherlands or US  (Van Rompay-Bartels & Geessink, 2021).

### 3.3 Proxy Constructs & Adjacent Scales

- **Face-Negotiation Theory:** Ting-Toomey's work quantifies self-face vs. other-face concern across US, China, Taiwan, Japan, Korea—mapping onto direct/indirect tendencies  (Ting-Toomey et al., 1991).
- **High/Low Context Communication:** Empirical scales confirm Hall's continuum: US is low-context/direct; China/Japan are high-context/indirect  (Vescovi et al., 2022).
- **Schwartz Values:** Autonomy (vs. embeddedness) at the societal level aligns with preference for directness in communication  (Ralston et al., 2011; Heim et al., 2017).

### 3.4 Limitations & Data Quality

- Some indices (e.g., Hofstede) are based on older data but remain widely used due to lack of alternatives  (Beugelsdijk et al., 2015).
- Measurement invariance issues exist when comparing feedback constructs across highly diverse cultures  (De Vries et al., 2022).

## 4. Discussion

The strongest available quantitative proxies for Meyer's Evaluating scale are the GLOBE Assertiveness scores and tight-loose indices—both provide country rankings that can be normalized to a 0–99 scale for database construction  (House et al., 2002; Bonjeer & Vonkova, 2023; Terlutter et al., 2010; Rafferty, 2025; Moneim, 2007; Uz, 2015; Gelfand et al., 2011). While these do not measure negative feedback delivery directly, assertiveness closely tracks willingness to give blunt criticism; tightness indexes capture indirectness via norm enforcement and avoidance of confrontation.

Qualitative studies consistently show Americans/Germans/Dutch as highly direct in giving negative feedback; East Asians (Japan/Korea/China), Middle Easterners (Persia/Iran), and Southeast Asians are more indirect due to face-saving norms and hierarchical structures  (Fullerton et al., 2025; Haristiani et al., 2023; Hosseinizadeh & Moqadam, 2019; Safari, 2020). Peer-reviewed speech act analyses allow relative positioning even without numeric values.

Proxy metrics such as Hofstede Individualism or Schwartz Autonomy can supplement gaps but should be used cautiously due to conceptual overlap rather than perfect alignment with Meyer's construct  (Akaliyski et al., 2025; Minkov & Kaasa, 2022; Ralston et al., 2011). High/low context scales also provide useful ordinal rankings but lack fine-grained numeric precision.

## 5. Conclusion

Empirical ranking of countries on Meyer's Evaluating scale is best achieved by normalizing GLOBE Assertiveness scores or tight-loose indices; qualitative studies fill gaps where numeric data is lacking. Proxy metrics like Hofstede Individualism or Schwartz Autonomy offer supplementary insights but should not replace primary measures.

### Open Research Questions

Future research should focus on developing more precise measurement tools that directly assess negative feedback delivery styles across diverse national contexts.

|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **How well do GLOBE Assertiveness scores predict actual negative feedback delivery styles?**      | Direct validation would improve confidence in using these scores as proxies for Meyer's Evaluating scale.         |
| **Can new large-scale surveys directly measure negative feedback preferences by country?**        | Direct measurement would eliminate reliance on proxies and improve accuracy of cross-cultural rankings.            |
| **How do digital communication platforms affect cultural differences in giving critical feedback?**     | As remote work grows globally, understanding shifts in evaluating styles is crucial for business effectiveness.     |
