# Building a **0–99 Trusting** Index

Erin Meyer’s **Trusting** scale can be approximated empirically, but no paper here gives a direct country-by-country 0–99 “task-based vs relationship-based” score. The strongest route is to build a composite from cross-national **trust radius** and **generalized trust** measures, then use **particularism/collectivism/kinship** variables as secondary proxies, and reserve qualitative business-culture studies for gap-filling only.

## Best Direct Measures

The closest empirical match is the **trust radius** literature, because Meyer’s contrast is not simply “how much people trust,” but **who must be known first** before trust extends beyond close ties  (Delhey et al., 2011). Delhey, Newton, and Welzel explicitly define general trust as the product of the **level** of trust and the **radius** of trust, show that the radius varies substantially across countries, and report that some country rankings change dramatically once trust is radius-adjusted  (Delhey et al., 2011).

That makes radius-adjusted trust a better primary input than the standard WVS “most people can be trusted” item alone, because the standard item can overstate broad trust where respondents mainly mean a narrow circle  (Delhey et al., 2011). This matters especially for Confucian contexts: they can report relatively high trust yet still have a **narrow radius**, meaning strong trust inside relationships but weaker default trust outside them  (Delhey et al., 2011).

A second strong numeric source is generalized-trust country data from WVS-based compilations. Freitag and Bühlmann provide country-level generalized trust values for 58 countries, with very high scores in Scandinavia and much lower scores in parts of Africa, Latin America, the Philippines, and Romania  (Freitag & Bühlmann, 2009). Paldam likewise reports that country generalized-trust values are listed for 83 countries in the WVS appendices, giving a broad numeric base if raw appendices are available  (Paldam, 2011).

A third useful numeric source is the Global Preference Survey, which measures trust in 76 countries using an experimentally validated survey module and documents substantial between-country heterogeneity  (Falk et al., 2018). Enke also notes that the GPS trust item is significantly negatively correlated with kinship tightness, which helps bridge a pure trust metric to a relationship-based social structure metric  (Enke, 2017).

## Quantitative Proxies

If direct trust-radius data are missing, the best proxies are **kinship tightness**, **collectivism**, and related measures of in-group favoritism. Enke shows that kinship tightness is positively associated with trust in neighbors but negatively associated with generalized trust and trust in strangers or foreigners, and strongly correlates with the gap between in-group and out-group trust  (Enke, 2018). That is conceptually very close to Meyer’s relationship-based end.

This proxy is stronger than simple Hofstede-style individualism alone because it directly targets the **in-group vs out-group trust split** rather than broader self-construal  (Enke, 2017). Related evidence converges: societies with loose kinship ties trust broadly, while tightly knit kinship societies distrust out-groups but support in-group members  (Enke, 2018).

Collectivism also tracks narrower extension of trust. Glanville and Shi find that in more collectivist countries, particularized trust extends less strongly into generalized and out-group trust  (Glanville & Shi, 2019). Pelham and colleagues provide a Global Collectivism Index with near-global coverage and explicit in-group favoritism sub-scores, which makes it a practical large-coverage proxy when trust-radius data are absent  (Pelham et al., 2021).

GLOBE variables are usable, but with caution. GLOBE covers 61 nations and distinguishes **institutional collectivism** from **in-group collectivism**  (House et al., 2002). For Meyer’s Trusting dimension, **in-group collectivism** is conceptually closer than institutional collectivism, but Brewer and Venaik argue GLOBE’s in-group collectivism is best read as **family collectivism**, not a broad national trust measure  (Brewer & Venaik, 2011).

## Qualitative and Semi-Quantitative Sources

The most on-the-nose conceptual proxy is Trompenaars’ **universalism–particularism** dimension, because it asks whether rules generalize across contexts or whether **relationships and circumstances** override them  (Taras, 2015; Cviklova, 2022). That maps well to Meyer’s task-based versus relationship-based trust. The problem is measurement quality: Hofstede’s reanalysis concluded Trompenaars’ database clearly confirmed only two dimensions, raised concerns about methodology, and argued the book’s theory was not supported by the database  (Hofstede, 1996).

That means Trompenaars is useful as **qualitative ordering**, not as a primary numeric backbone. Smaller studies still recover usable relative positions. For example, Moldovan managers are described as **particularist** and as having interconnected personal relationships, which is evidence toward the relationship-based side even though it is not a cross-national cardinal score  (Pîrlog, 2022). A Turkish study using executives and Meyer’s own framework also places Türkiye away from the midpoint on the Trusting dimension, though it remains qualitative and non-generalizable  (Dinçer et al., 2023).

Business-relationship literatures around **guanxi, yongo, and wasta** are also valuable interpretive anchors. Cross-country survey work comparing China, South Korea, Jordan, and Egypt validates comparable relationship constructs across these settings and shows that trustworthiness and reciprocity are measured differently across guanxi/yongo versus wasta systems  (Zhang et al., 2021). These studies do not rank countries globally, but they help calibrate what the relationship-based extreme looks like in practice  (Zhang et al., 2021).

## Recommended Scoring Recipe

A defensible database should use a layered scoring design rather than pretending one observed variable equals Meyer’s scale. The primary score should be based on **radius-adjusted trust** where available, because it directly captures whether trust reaches beyond known ties  (Delhey et al., 2011).

A practical formula is: first standardize each direct numeric source to 0–99; second, orient the direction so higher values mean more **relationship-based** trust; third, combine sources with weights that favor trust radius over plain generalized trust over generic trust-preference measures. This is justified because the literature distinguishes generalized trust from particularized trust and shows that comparisons improve when multiple trust domains are separated  (Freitag & Bauer, 2013).

For countries missing direct trust-radius data, impute from proxies in descending order: **kinship tightness**, **GCI collectivism/in-group favoritism**, **GLOBE in-group collectivism**, **universalism–particularism**, and then qualitative business-relationship evidence such as guanxi/wasta/yongo prevalence  (Enke, 2017; Pelham et al., 2021; House et al., 2002; Lee et al., 2017). This ordering matches the evidence strength and construct proximity.

## Key Caveats

The main limitation is construct mismatch. Meyer’s “task-based vs relationship-based” trust is a **business-interaction construct**, while most high-quality country datasets measure **generalized social trust**, trust radius, or collectivist moral boundaries rather than business trust specifically  (Freitag & Bühlmann, 2009; Zou et al., 2023).

A second limitation is that some popular proxy datasets are conceptually unstable. Brewer and Venaik argue Hofstede’s individualism and GLOBE’s in-group collectivism are often misread, and Hofstede’s critique of Trompenaars raises serious validity concerns for universalism–particularism as a numeric country league table  (Brewer & Venaik, 2011; Hofstede, 1996).

A third limitation is that trust itself is multidimensional. Comparative work finds that social trust is better represented as **particularized, identity-based, and generalized** trust rather than one single factor, and WVS items like “most people” and “people met for the first time” only correlate moderately at the society level  (Freitag & Bauer, 2013; Hamamura, 2012). That is exactly why a composite index is safer than any single source.

## Bottom Line

For a country database mapped to Erin Meyer’s **Trusting** scale, the best primary source is **radius-adjusted trust** from Delhey, Newton, and Welzel, backed by WVS generalized-trust tables and GPS trust scores. The best secondary proxies are **kinship tightness** and **collectivism/in-group favoritism**. Trompenaars-style **particularism** and guanxi/wasta/yongo studies are useful for interpretation, but too weak to serve as the core numeric backbone.

The single most important shift in the evidence is from measuring **how much** people trust to measuring **how widely** they trust. The biggest open question is how well these broad social-trust metrics map onto **business-specific** task-based versus relationship-based trust across countries.
